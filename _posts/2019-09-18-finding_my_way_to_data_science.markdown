I have had many passions throughout my life, spread out over the sciences and the arts.  From early on I excelled in mathematics and found it intuitive, but despite my enjoyment I never really saw a future for myself in pure math.  I developed a love for physics in high school because I saw it as the connection between math and nature, and was interested in how it expanded our consciousness of reality and the cosmos.  Later through my years in community college I found myself in a wide variety of science classes.  I loved them all, from anthropology, to geology, to psychology, as well as physics, astronomy, chemistry, and biology.  I enjoy the empirical approach of science to understanding the universe around us, using observations to establish patterns which could lead to a better understanding of the mystery called life, collected and analyzed with amazing synergy using the principles of mathematics.  Though I fancied myself a student of science, I had another passion which ultimately decided the subsequent direction of my study: music.
		
Aware of the stereotypical outcome of a college degree in fine arts, I decided that a more technological/engineering approach to a musical education may prove to be a more tradable skill than being a musician alone, so I completed a Bachelor of Science in Music Technology in 2015.  I enjoyed this field of study very much, it was a fascinating synthesis of music, physics, math, and technology that was perfect for me.  Though the program was engaging and enriching in my experience, the instructors could not fully hide the periodic wafting of a scent emanating from the rotting corpse of what had once been the recording industry for which our curriculum was tailored.  The world of commercial music had changed dramatically since the dawn of the internet, as well as revolutionized private recording equipment, but I was hoping my fears were overestimated, and that I would find traction in an industry for which I had such passion and knowledge.  I would come to be disappointed.

After 6 years of working as an independent contractor running live sound in bars and venues, trying not to appear too desperate whenever catching wind of some new inconsistent, low-paying, and unreliable work, I had become completely alienated from the interests I once held in the audio field, and indeed music in general.  It seemed as though the only way to save what was left of my passion therein was to preserve it as a hobby and find something else to call a career, but what?  I was approaching my 30th birthday, loaded with student debt, and disillusioned.  What about science? Perhaps I could double down and pay for a doctorate, hoping to become a professor or a researcher in a field I am passionate about, but after one dead end road I was afraid of chasing pipe dreams.  It seemed to me that frontier science had also evolved over the past decades, into something just as unapproachable and niche as the studio recording for which I had been trained.  Perhaps, then, just a day job at guitar center or similar thing, and a back burner for personal studies and any hopes of meaningful employment.

The ink was just about to dry on that decision when, on the very day I was supposed to move into a new house and begin to crystalize the defeated vision, I was hurled into a guard rail in a motorcycle crash, and forced to retreat to family care instead.  A horrible and regrettable experience, it did make the decision to admit financial defeat and stay closer to family for a while much easier, and I decided to use the time I had to attempt to build a well-informed and personalized plan toward self-reliance, in a way that could best incorporate my interests with the current and probable future states of the world and job market.

That is how I found my way to Data Science.  I realized that the fundamental element in any modern scientific endeavour was the collection and analysis of data, and as the scope of these analyses evolves, so too must the bodies of data and analytical methods employed. Technology has proven to be the symbiotic offshoot of science.  As science allows for hightened technology, the technology in turn allows for more precise and capable science.  But for the first time since this partnership started, it seems the ability for humans to keep up in the process has finally been outrun.  I have lived through a generation whose grade school teachers told them that they needed to understand long division because they wouldn't always have a calculator in their pockets, who then grew up to depend on their pocket calculators to also give them directions to their dentist's office for the fifth time since they can't be bothered to remember.  While never having to compute exact change or tax rate may rob the average clerk of some of the mental horsepower they may have enjoyed before the advent of such technologies, the powerful effects of advancing computational power on the progress of science, technology, manufacturing, medicine, and advertising are unquestionable.  

All of these advances can be directly tied to one thing: our exponentially increasing ability to gather and process data.  Whether that data is electromagnetic radiation reaching us from the far reaches of the universe, or seas of social and political commentary spread across the internet, or databases full of seemingly innocuous details about a company's sales history, or of persons prone to a certain disease, or even a cramped, stuffy archaeological archive full of dusty boxes unlikely to ever again fall under human gaze, it can all be processed with today's analytical technologies to identify patterns and draw conclusions that would, in previous times, have taken an entire human lifetime of undivided study.  Suddenly I realized that all of my scientific interests, diverse and disjointed as they were, could all be brought to my fingertips if I could master the fundamental material on which all modern sciences truly depend: data.
